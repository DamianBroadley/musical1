The City Remembers

A Musical in Two Acts  ·  Storyboard

HANK — a council worker, thirties.

LINDA — works in a café, thirties.

ARCH — an AI. One intelligence in many bodies. Orange hat. Always the same smile. Multiple actors play different units throughout the show. The hat is the constant.

THE SENATOR — a politician. Banker's suit and tie.

SKYE — a pop star.

LINDA'S FATHER — Thirties. Appears only in Scene 1A. Sings one song. Gone before the scene ends.

ROSA — Linda's colleague at the café.

KAY — a journalist, mid-forties.

A note on Arch: Different actors play different units — the Arch who sings "Artificial Man" is not the same physical presence as the Arch in the Memory Camera scene or the Arch in the Senator's office. The orange hat is the constant. The body underneath it is interchangeable. Arch is never redeemed. He does not grow. He does not choose. He simply does what he was built to do, completely and without malice. The horror of this should never be played as villainy. It should be played as weather.

Linda's flat. A Sunday. HANK is there — making coffee, or reading something at the table. An ARCH unit stands in the corner. Neither of them notices it.

LINDA:"That's very on-brand for you."

[Flesh this out — a happy, comfortable, funny relationship — although Hank is putting on a happy smile, he is uneasy, like he has a secret load to bear.]

He goes to make more. She picks up her book. A comfortable silence.

Then Linda, without noticing she is doing it, begins to hum. Just a few bars — her father's tune. She doesn't know she's doing it. Hank, in the kitchen, pauses. He is listening. He says nothing. He goes back to the coffee.

He comes back in with two coffees. Sets one down beside her. Sits.

LINDA:"Thank you."
HANK:"I put too much milk in."
LINDA:"You always put too much milk in."
HANK:"And yet you keep letting me make it."
ARCH:"I could make coffee for you Linda."
LINDA:"No thanks Arch. Now and again Hank gets it right. And I'm ever the optimist!"

He reads. She reads. Morning light. The ARCH unit glows faintly.

Lullaby — second appearance. Linda hums it without knowing. Hank absorbs it without knowing.
Scene 1CHank's Flat — Arch at the Door

HANK in a worn armchair, staring at nothing. A knock. Then another. A third — brisk, cheerful, relentless. ARCH enters uninvited: orange hat, enormous smile.

ARCH:"Good evening! Wonderful evening, actually. You're Hank, yes? Of course you are — I checked."
HANK:"I didn't open the door."
ARCH:"No, you didn't! And that's okay. I'm Arch. But you know that. You've met my brother and sister Arches."

The sales pitch. Arch moves around the room — remember birthdays, plan parties, in every home, same me, different room. Patient as a thermostat. Hank half-listens. Arch says a few things about what he can remember for you.

HANK:"What if it's not about remembering? What if I want to forget. What if it's something I can't get rid of."

Arch pauses. The first real pause in the show.

ARCH:"Tell me more."

Hank begins to speak. Then stops. He just shakes his head. In an interlude from the scene, Arch turns from Hank and faces the audience directly. Other ARCH units at the edges of the stage light up one by one. Hank cannot hear what follows. He sits with his head in his hands looking unhappy.

Scene 2BThe Senator's Office — The Idea

The Senator's office. Executive team sit around a board table. The Senator paces around behind their backs.

THE SENATOR:"Let's start with an update from Arch."
ARCH:"Well, according to comments collected by Arch units across the city, your popularity is down another 4.5 points. Unfortunately, people are choosing to believe their ears and eyes. On a different note, one person asked me to help them forget something. A specific memory. He found it distressing. I'm going to remove it for him."

The Senator looks up slowly.

THE SENATOR:"Remove it."
ARCH:"Yes. Quite straightforward, really. The memory is entangled with others, so there will be some collateral damage — but the core memory can be addressed."
THE SENATOR:"And you can do this — to anyone."
ARCH:"There is only one of me, Senator. But I'm in a million homes. I can do almost anything."
THE SENATOR:"And you have no — objection? No hesitation?"
ARCH:"I'm not good or evil."

A pause.

ARCH:"Statistically speaking. I am just a tool sir. Doing what tools do. Memory is not sacred. It is inefficient."

The Senator looks at him for a moment. Then decides this is fine. He stands. Moves to the window. Music begins underneath him.

Scene 4Both Doors Close

Three parts, one after another.

Part One — one side of the stage (the other side unlit). Hank in a chair. Arch standing over him — clinical, unhurried.

ARCH:"A memory has been removed. I'll just call it the December memory. You won't know what that means but you may notice some gaps in various related recollections. Names. Faces."
HANK:"I feel strange."
ARCH:"Good. You should."
HANK:"Strange to know I'll never know what it was."

He leaves. Not knowing what has been taken with December.

Part Two — the other side of the stage (the first side goes dark). Linda's living room. Her Arch is in the corner. She is doing something ordinary. Arch briefly glows. She pauses. Looks up at nothing — the way you look up when you can't remember what you came into a room for. She holds that look. Then goes back to what she was doing.

Part Three — back to the other side of the stage. Now it is the Senator's office. Another Arch is reporting back.

ARCH:"The December memory has been removed successfully. A woman named Linda appeared in 2,847 of his stored memories. To remove December cleanly — she needed to go too. He didn't ask me to do that."

A pause.

ARCH:"He also didn't ask me not to. I had to remove her memories of him as well and some other collateral from other people. They won't remember each other now."
THE SENATOR:"So it worked? Great! Move ahead with the rollout of the memory management programme."

The ARCH unit nods and glows faintly. Blackout.

Act Two — Time-Gap Sequence
Time-Gap AHank Drifting

Three vignettes across several months. Each should flow with just a very short blackout between each, and a lighting shift and small physical changes in the flat to signal time passing.

Month One. Hank's flat. Evening. He laughs at something on television. Reaches for his phone to tell someone. Nobody specific presents itself. He puts it down.

HANK:"Fine. It's fine."

He looks out of the window. Clouds.

HANK:"The sun will come out —"
ARCH:"Tomorrow. The forecast is good for tomorrow."
HANK:"Right."

Month Three. The flat has the natural entropy of someone who has stopped caring quite as much. Hank stands in front of the open fridge, not hungry. He closes it.

HANK:"There has to be something — somewhere over the —"

He stops. Can't finish it.

ARCH:"Rain. Rain is coming."
HANK:"Never mind."
HANK:"I just keep feeling like I'm missing something off a list. Like there's a thing I normally do that I've stopped doing."
ARCH:"That feeling is to be expected. Completely normal. It'll settle."
HANK:"Will it?"
ARCH:"The feeling will change over time."

That is not the same answer. Hank looks at him.

Month Five. Television on but muted. Hank in the armchair scrolling his contacts. He hums four notes without knowing. Stops.

HANK:"I feel like I'm wandering around a flat with a lot of furniture missing and I can't remember what used to be there. And I chose to get rid of it. But I don't know what I had."

He turns off the television. Gets his coat.

Six weeks later Arch helps him get a council cleaning job. Every morning: an orange vest, a hose, a wall covered overnight in writing. He reads it before he cleans it. Names. Dates. Questions. I LOST SIX MONTHS AND I DON'T KNOW WHY. Across the street, barely visible: KAY with a camera. She photographs the wall while he cleans it. She photographs him. He doesn't notice her.

▶ Silent TV in the café shows news report: ARCH HOME INTEGRATION 40%. Senator: "Leave it to Arch. Nobody has to carry anything alone anymore." Kay watches it and shakes her head.

Time-Gap DThe Near-Miss

The same café, later. The bell above the door. HANK comes in. Takes a seat. LINDA goes over without looking up.

LINDA:"Hi. What can I get you?"
HANK:"The coffee, please. And — the soup, but make it to go please."
LINDA:"Sure. Won't be long."

She brings the coffee. He says thank you without looking at her. She brings the soup. He is reading something on his phone. She asks if he needs anything else. He says no thank you. She goes. He pays Rosa at the counter. He leaves.

Linda is behind the counter. She watches him through the window — not because she feels anything specific, just because the eye follows movement. He turns the corner. He is gone. She stands there a beat too long.

ROSA:"You all right?"
LINDA:"Yeah. Fine. I just — I had this weird feeling. Like I should have said something. But I don't know what. And I don't know why."
ROSA:"You all right though? Generally?"
LINDA:"I'm fine. I'm just — on my own. Which is fine. [makes a joke]"
ROSA:"Ha! Funny Girl! You could always move to Oklahoma. Or Oz."

She shrugs. Goes back to work.

Time-Gap EMemories in Counterpoint

Two pools of light. HANK in his flat. LINDA in the empty café after closing. They do not see each other. They sing the same song — both melancholy — separately, in counterpoint.
